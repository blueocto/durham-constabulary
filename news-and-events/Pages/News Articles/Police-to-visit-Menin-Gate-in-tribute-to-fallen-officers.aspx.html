 Police to visit Menin Gate in tribute to fallen officers

 05/04/2018

Page Image
Page Content

​FALLEN police officers who served in the First World War will be honoured in a poignant remembrance ceremony in Europe this weekend.


Officers and staff from Durham Constabulary are travelling to Ypres, in Belgium, to take part in The Last Post ceremony at the Menin Gate on Friday evening.


Chief Inspector Catherine Clarke, Inspector Ed Turner, PC Glen Henderson and retired PC Dave Cuthbertson will parade the force standard during the event and lay a wreath to remember those police officers who lost their lives during the war. 


The Last Post ceremony has taken place every evening since November 1929. From 7.30pm, the roads surrounding the Menin Gate come to standstill and the area falls silent as people gather to pay their respects to those who fell during battle.


At 8pm, the Last Post sounds before a minute’s silence is held, with wreaths laid afterwards.


Over three days, they will also visit the graves of 20 former Durham Constabulary officers who are buried or remembered in memorials in the Ypres, Somme and Arras sectors, where individual tributes will be left to each of the officers.


The trip has been based on extensive research into fallen Durham Constabulary officers carried out by former police officer John Grainger. 


Ch Insp Clarke said: “As this year marks the centenary of the end of the First World War, we wanted to pay tribute to those who made an extraordinary sacrifice. 


“This trip started as an idea following the Remembrance Festival and Parade that we were privileged to take part in last year, and as it developed we became aware of some research completed by a retired Durham Constabulary officer about former officers from the force who had gone to fight in the Great War.


“In addition to laying a wreath on behalf of the constabulary in the Last Post ceremony at the Menin Gate, this research has enabled us to plan our battlefields tour to take an individually named tribute to as many of our former colleagues as possible who were laid to rest or commemorated in the cemeteries and memorials of the area.


“In the 100th anniversary year of the end of the Great War we feel a huge sense of pride and gratitude to our former colleagues who made the ultimate sacrifice.”


Assistant Chief Constable Dave Orford said: “It is extremely important that we remember the sacrifice of those who gave their lives for our freedom. The Last Post ceremony is a very poignant and emotive event and I know our officers feel extremely humbled to be taking part.”

 

The full list of officers who will be remembered during the visit are:
 
William Ashburn
Bluet Farm Cemetery
28/7/1885 - 26/9/1917
Awarded British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Served in Northumberland County Constabulary before joining Durham on January 13, 1913 and was recruited to Southwick. He was later transferred to Silksworth and the East Herrington before resigning on June 25, 1915 to join the Royal Engineers as a Sapper.
He transferred to the York and Lancaster Regiment on September 13, 1917 for the Battle of Passchendaele.
He was killed in action in Belgium by shellfire on September 26.
 
Bertie John James Ball
Thiepval Memorial
25/9/1888 – 8/11/1916
Awarded 1914 – 1915 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Joined Durham County Constabulary on February 4, 1915 and recruited to Darlington.
He the Military Foot Police on June 16, 1915 where he was appointed Lance Corporal.
He entered France on July 26, 1915 where he was transferred to the 2nd Battalion Devonshire Regiment.   
He was killed in action in France on November 8, 1916 aged 28.
 
George Brown
Menin Gate
7/6/1890 – 11/11/1914
Awarded 1914 Star with clasp, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Previously served as a police constable with Liverpool Police before voluntarily resigning and enlisting with the Scots Guards and serving Egpyt.
Joined Durham County Constabulary on June 15, 1914 and was stationed in Bishop Auckland.
Brown was recalled to re-join his regiment on August 5, 1914 and entered France on August 27 that year.
He was involved in the thick of fighting right from the beginning of the war.
He died on November 11, 1914 in Belgium during heavy shelling that began at 6.30am and lasted for three hours, during which time all trenches and dugouts were knocked in. The enemy got within 200 yards of guns but were driven back – it was during this action that Brown was killed, aged 24.
 
Thomas Brunskill
Vlamertinghe New Military Cemetery, Ypres
20/5/1889 – 4/12/1917
Awarded 1914 – 1915 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Born in Middleton One Row, Brunskill joined Durham County Constabulary on November 19, 1910 as a 3rd class constable. He lived at Darlington police station with ten other constables before being transferred to Sacriston in June 1912 as a 2nd class constable and then became a 1st class constable. He resigned to join the Military Foot Police on June 16, 1915.
Brunskill entered France on August 25, 1915. He was killed in action at Asylum Corner, Ypres, aged 28 on December 4, 1917. He was on duty in charge of a number of stores when a shell burst and he was killed.
 
 
Ralph Carrol
Thiepval Memorial
23/3/1886 – 19/8/1916
Awarded 1914 – 1915 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Born in Sunderland, he joined Durham County Constabulary on December 14, 1907. When he applied for the police service he was an army reservist with the Royal Garrison Artillery.
He was recalled to the colours to re-join his regiment on August 4, 1914 and entered France on August 21, 1915.
He died from his wounds on August 19, 1916 in France aged 30.
 
Stephen Coulson Clarke
Thiepval Memorial
7/6/1887 – 14-16/9/1916
Awarded British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Born in Carlton, Stokesley, Clarke joined Durham County Constabulary on January 20, 1913 and was recruited to Hebburn before transferring to Stanley.
He resigned and joined HMF on May 17, 1915 as part of the Coldstream Guards and entered France on April 6, 1916.
He was involved in the Battle of Ginchy during the Battle of the Somme where he was killed in action between September 14 and September 16, 1916.
 
Frederick Hazell
Euston Roadd Cemetery, Colincamps
August 1885 – 18/8/1916
Awarded 1914 Star and clasp, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Originally from Newbury, in Berkshire, Hazell attested for service with the Coldstream Guards on December 20, 1905.
He was appointed to Durham County Constabulary on December 10, 1913 and worked in Sacriston.
Hazell was recalled to the colours to re-join his regiment on August 6, 1914.
He entered France on August 12, 1914. He was killed in action with the 2nd Battalion on August 18, 1916 near Lebuterne.
 
John William Hunter
Buried Buffs Road Cemetery
15/12/1891 – 31/7/1917
Awarded British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Joined Durham County Constabulary on July 21, 1914 and recruited to Stockton-on-Tees.
Resigned to join HMF on July 31, 1915 and was enlisted as a Sapper in the Royal Engineers 234th Field Company.
He entered France on March 8, 1916 where his company were at first posted to the section of front known as ‘Rotton Row’ to ‘Bond Street.’ Throughout the war they constructed and repaired trenches, built observation posts and machine gun emplacements, and support infantry raids to blow up captured trenches and dugouts.
He was killed in action on July 31, 1917 in Flanders on the first day of the Battle of Pilckem Ridge.
 
Alexander Johnston
Arras Memorial
3/5/1891 – 25/3/1918
Awarded 1914 – 1915 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
He joined Durham County Constabulary on September 16, 1912 as a PC where he worked as a finance clerk for the Chief Constables office at HQ, in Durham.
He resigned on September 3, 1914 to join HMF as Private 17866 Hussars of the Line.
Johnston embarked for France on May 18, 1915. He later received a commission as 2nd Lieutenant, East Yorkshire Regiment.
He was killed in action in France on March 25, 1918.
 
Thomas William McGuire
Pozieres Memorial
9/9/1881 – 26/3/1918
Awarded British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Joined Durham County Constabulary on May 15, 1905 and was posted to Darlington before being transferred to Silksworth. In September 1912 he was transferred to West Hartlepool before resigning in September 1915, when he joined the Durham Light Infantry.
In March 1918, his battalion were in the Ypres area assisting with the construction of dugouts, strong points and machine gun posts when they were placed on five hours standby to move due to the German offensive in France.
Over the next few days the battalion made their way to the Rosieres area where they were involved in heavy fighting.
They suffered more than 400 casualties, one of them being McGuire whose body was not found.
 
John Fletcher Nicholson
Vlamertinghe New Military Cemetery
29/1/1885 – 31/7/1917
Awarded British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Joined Durham County Constabulary on January 7, 1914 and was recruited to West Hartlepool.
Resigned on May 31, 1915 to join HMF as part of the Royal Engineers 225th Field Company.
In July 1917, the company was involved in the Battle of Pilckem Ridge, during which Nicholson was killed in action.
 
James William Noutch
Menin Gate
18/5/1884 - 30/10/1914
Awarded 1914 Star and clasp, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Served with the Grenadier Guards between 1902 and 1910, before joining Durham County Constabulary on December 10, 1910 where he lived in Darlington Police Station with ten other constables.
Recalled to the colours to re-join his regiment on August 4, 1914 and entered France on September 12 that same year.
He was involved in battle near Polygon Wood and was killed in action on October 30, 1914.
 
Thomas Nugent
Menin Gate
7/1/1888 – 1/11/1914
Awarded 1914 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Appointed to Durham County Constabulary on April 15, 1913 and recruited to Blaydon after serving with the Irish Guards.
Recalled to the colours to re-join his regiment on August 4, 1914 and entered France on August 12.
Reported missing in action on November 1, before being declared killed in action in Belgium.
 
Hugh Laing Ritson
Bailleul Road East Cemetery
12/2/1893 – 9/4/1917
Awarded British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Joined Durham County Constabulary on March 16, 1914 and recruited to Consett before being transferred to West Stanley.
He resigned to joined HMF on June 21, 1915 and enlisted as a Private with the Northumberland Fusiliers.
He was killed in action in France on April 9, 1917, aged 24.
 
William Stevenson
Sanctuary Wood Cemetery
29/11/1883 - 2/3/1916
Awarded 1914 – 1915 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Having previously served with the Northumberland Fusiliers, he joined Durham County Constabulary on May 13, 1907 where he was recruited to Darlington.
He resigned on March 23, 1915 to join HMF and was enlisted as a Private in the Gordon Highlanders, attached to the 3rd Battalion.
He was killed in action in Flanders on March 2, 1916 aged 32.
 
James Turnbull Tracey
Menin Gate
5/5/1889 – 2/11/1914
Awarded 1914 Star and clasp, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Born in Sunderland, Tracey attested for service with the Coldstream Guards on January 24, 1910 aged 20.
Joined Durham County Constabulary on January 23, 1913 and was recruited to Seaham Harbour before being transferred to Houghton-le-Spring.
Recalled to colours to re-join his regiment on August 6, 1914 with the 2nd Battalion and entered France on August 12.
He was killed in action on November 11 1914 in Rental, Belgium, aged 25 after his Battalion came under considerable rifle fire.
 
Joseph Trotter
Arras Memorial
22/10/1879 - 27/3/1918
Awarded Queen’s South Africa Medal, 1914 – 1915 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Originally from Worcester, Trotter previously served in the Boer War as a Private with the Grenadier Guards.
He joined Durham County Constabulary on April 9, 1903 and was recruited to Darlington before being transferred to Lanchester on June 1, 1905. He was commended by the Chief Constable on June 30, 1906 for pluck and promptitude displayed when stopping a runaway horse.
Trotter resigned to join HMF on March 16, 1915 as a Private in the Grenadier Guards.
Entered France on May 24, 1915 with the 2nd Battalion where he was twice wounded in action.
He was killed in action on March 27, 1918 in France aged 38 after his Battalion suffered heavy shelling and was attacked by machine gun fire and mortar fire causing considerable casualties.
 
Joseph Turton
Arras Memorial
29/2/1884 – 21/3/1918
Awarded Military Medal, 1914 Star, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Born in Hetton-le-Hole, Turton joined in Durham Light Infantry as a Private on August 16, 1904. Aged 20.
He joined Durham County Constabulary on October 15, 1907 and was posted to Darlington the following year.
He was recalled to the colours to re-join his regiment on August 5, 1914 and entered France on September, 8 where he was twice promoted on the field for gallant conduct.
He was killed in action in France on March 21, 1918 aged 33.
 
 
Barnes Usherwood
Menin Gate
21/7/1889 – 9/11/1914
Awarded 1914 Star and clasp, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Born in Kent, he attested for service with the East Kent Regiment in 1909 aged 20, before joining the Grenadier Guards the following year.
He was appointed to Durham County Constabulary on April 22, 1913 and recruited to West Hartlepool.
Recalled to the colours to re-join his regiment on August 4, 1914 and entered France the following month with the Grenadier Guards.
Usherwood died from wounds sustained when he was hit by shrapnel during a bombardment on November 9, 1914 in Belgium aged 25.
 
 
Henry Ward
Perth Cemetery
21/3/1874 – 23/10/1914
Awarded Queen’s South Africa clasp Cape Colony and South Africa 1902, 1914 Star and clasp, British War Medal and Victory Medal.
Born in Framwellgate, Durham, he attested for service with the Coldstream Guards as a Private on September 20, 1898, aged 21.
He was appointed to Durham County Constabulary on January 16, 1903 and recruited to the Bishop Auckland division. He was stationed at Willington where he lived above the police station before moving to Low Spennymoor, Page Bank and Langley Moor.
Recalled to colours to re-join his regiment on August 5, 1914 and entered France on September that year.
He received a head wound during fighting at the Aisne in October 1914 and was admitted to a base hospital in France. He had just returned to the trenches when he was fatally wounded a few days later. He was 40 years old.
